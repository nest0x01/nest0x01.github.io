Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) Movement
A Brief Introduction
Oct. 2025

1. Informal Definition

Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) refers to software developed under principles that grant users the freedom to access, study, modify, and redistribute its source code. This paradigm enables researchers, developers, and organizations to adapt software to specific needs while contributing improvements to the wider community. FOSS thereby promotes transparency, collaborative innovation, and collective technological advancement.

2. History

Emerging in the 1970s from the hacker culture at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), the movement was formalized in 1983 when Richard Stallman of the MIT Artificial Intelligence Laboratory launched the GNU Project to counteract the growing restrictions imposed by proprietary software. These restrictions were transforming an open culture of collaboration and knowledge exchange into one of secrecy and limited autonomy.

The GNU Project aimed to develop a completely free, Unix-like operating system that would promote software freedom. In 1991, Finnish software engineer Linus Torvalds released Linux, which—combined with GNU software—formed the first fully functional open-source operating system, often referred to as GNU/Linux.

The term "open source", coined in 1998 by Eric S. Raymond and Bruce Perens through the Open Source Initiative (OSI), made the concept more business-friendly, diverging from Stallman's ethical stance.

Today, FOSS constitutes the foundation of digital infrastructure, powering web servers, mobile devices, supercomputers, the Darwin/XNU kernel of macOS, blockchain technologies, and even systems used aboard the International Space Station.

Despite its technical merits, GNU/Linux holds only about 3.2% of the desktop and laptop market share. This is largely due to Microsoft's aggressive and long-standing anticompetitive practices, including exclusionary licensing agreements, Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt (FUD) campaigns, and software lock-in mechanisms designed to undermine open-source adoption and suppress GNU/Linux’s growth in government and enterprise sectors. The strategy known as "embrace, extend, and extinguish" describes Microsoft's approach of adopting open standards, adding proprietary features to create incompatibility, and ultimately marginalizing competitors (United States v. Microsoft Corp., 2001).

3. Philosophy

FOSS are a shared public good, rejecting profit-driven enforced restrictions. Stallman’s four freedoms—to run, study, share, and modify—uphold freedom, collaboration, and autonomy (Stallman, 1983).

Eric Raymond's (1999) influential essay 'The Cathedral and the Bazaar' praised transparent, merit-based "bazaar" collaboration over hierarchical development models, blending altruism with reputation-based motivation.

FOSS is not only a technical paradigm but also a sociopolitical stance: information and knowledge should thrive without barriers.

4. Hackers' Culture

The Hackers’ culture prizes curiosity, the joy of tinkering, cleverness, and a playful approach to problem-solving. For example, GNU’s recursive acronym—“GNU’s Not Unix”—is itself a playful pun celebrating both technical lineage and mischievous creativity.

Hacker communities learn by exploring, experimenting, and openly sharing knowledge and discovery. Hackers view technology as a playground: they explore systems not just to use them, but to understand, modify, and repurpose them in unexpected ways.

Alongside the playfulness lies a serious ethic: respect for skill and technical excellence, and a belief in meritocracy in which reputation comes from what you can build or hack, not formal credentials.

5. Unix Heritage

GNU/Linux follows the philosophy of Unix, an operating system developed at Bell Laboratories in the late 1960s. The Unix design principles emphasize simple, modular programs that "do one thing well" and can be combined flexibly to accomplish complex tasks.

Initially adopted by universities, research institutions, and large corporations, Unix became one of the most influential operating systems in computing history. Its architectural philosophy profoundly shaped the design of modern operating systems, including GNU/Linux, BSD variants, and even proprietary systems such as macOS.
